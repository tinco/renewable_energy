On average, citizens of the UK travel 50 kilometers by car per day. This roughly equates to an energy consumption of 40kWh/d.

Wind energy

Energy can be drawn from the wind through windmills on land, in shallow waters and in deep waters.

If we would cover 10% of all land in the UK with wind farms, the wind farms would generate about 20kWh/d per person in total.

If we would cover all of the shallow waters around Britain with wind farms they would generate about 16kWh/d per person.

If we would cover all of the deep waters around Britain with wind farms they would generate another 32kWh/d per person.

Long travel

Airplane travel has become very common, and has a significant energy consumption impact.

One airplane holiday per year to a destination about 14.000KM away by plane costs around 12.000 KWh per passenger, so over a year this amounts to 33kWh/d per person.

Solar energy

Energy can be collected from the light that the Sun shines down during the day.

At home

The simplest and most efficient use of solar energy in cold countries is to warm water with it. If everyone would cover 10m2 of their south facing roofs with solar water heating panels then on average, this would yield about 13kWh/d per person, assuming 50% efficiency.

More expensive and less efficient, but more versatile, is converting the Sun's energy to electricity using photovoltaic panels. Theoretically the maximum efficiency of photovoltaic panels is 60%, when perfect lenses are used, and 45% without lenses. In practice no panels with an efficiency exceeding 23% exist in the market, and most panels have are about 14% efficient. If we would cover another 10m2 of south facing roofs with expensive 20% efficient panels, they would yield another 5kWh/d per person.

Solar farms

We could also use the land available around our cities to collect energy from the sun. If we would cover 5% of all land in the UK, or 200m2 per person, with 10% efficient panels, that would yield another 50kWh/d per person.

Another option is to cover the land with plants that could be used to generate energy, In Europe not many very efficient plants can grow. If we would cover 75% of the UK, or 3000m2 per person, with plants that generate 0.5W per square meter that would generate an additional 36kWh/d per person. This amount of land is equal to the amount of land currently reserved for agriculture.

Heating and cooling

Hot water for bathing, cooking, cleaning is estimated to cost about 12kWh/d per person.

Warming the air in the UK is estimated to cost about 24kWh/d per person on average.

Cooling air costs significant amounts of energy in countries where temperatures regularily exceed 30 degrees celsius. In the UK most temperatures rarely exceed 30 degrees, and thus cooling costs are quite small. We estimate an average of 1kWh/d per person, which also includes the refrigerator and freezer.

Hydro

Electricity can be generated from locations where rain water collects at attitude and can be collected behind dams. The north of the UK has enough of this altitude and rainfall to generate about 1.5kWh/d per person.

Also includes wave and tide

About this site

This site visualizes the total use of energy per person and contrast this with the means for production of energy. The style is inspired by the book Sustainable Energy — without the hot air.

The purpose of the site is to allow the visitor to explore the full story of energy consumption and production supported by actual information sources.

For simplicity, when the phrase "energy production" is used it is meant the process of collecting energy and transforming it to some form ready for storage or transport for consumption.

When the phrase "energy consumption" is used it is meant when energy is obtained from storage or transport and transformed into heat, light or motion.

This site regards all forms of energy consumption: direct —transforming stored energy into heat, light or motion— and indirect —using a service that is enabled by energy consumption—.

Insight is provided to what actions in the daily lives of persons result in energy consumption, and what impact they have both economically and on the environment.

The site regards both sustainable and less sustainable forms of energy production.

Insight is provided to what ways energy is and can be produced for consumption, and what impact those ways have both economically and on the environment.

All information on this site will be backed up with live links to credible sources — be they scientific research, industrial reports, investigative journalism, coverage of real life events or surveys.

The user of this site will be able to interact with the information to experiment or make it reflect their perspective and gain an understanding of how energy consumption is a part of their lives, and how energy is or could be produced for their use.
